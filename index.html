Our journey begins in Atacama Desert, Chile

The desert is is extremely dry. In fact, the Atacama Desert is the driest non-polar desert in the world. It's located at a very high altitude: 5000 ft., in order for ALMA to capture higher quality images.

Located in the Atacama Desert is ALMA (Atacama Large Millimeter Array).
ALMA needs to stay dry because it is made of types of Millimeter telescopes or Radio telescopes. Moisture can absorb the radio waves ALMA tries to measure. (Graph not to scale)

ALMA is a collection of 66 radio telescopes. Radio telescopes must be far away from everything else, since radio and TV waves can disrupt them. Radio telescopes work by capturing radiation from objects. Radiation is released by bodies in space such as stars and galaxies.

By having 66 telescopes, the pictures taken are more accurate and have much higher resolution. Each telescope is 115 tons (1 ton = 2200 pounds). One telescope is almost as heavy as 64 cars! These telescopes are often moved across the desert to get better images. It’s similar to “zooming in” to an object in the sky.

Watch the video to see how much it takes to move just one telescope!

ALMA is a project between the US, Canada, Asia, Europe and Chile. It cost $1.4 BILLION dollars and is the biggest, most expensive astronomical project on ground.

ALMA is amazing because it will help scientists to study new stars, planets and even other galaxies. ALMA is one of the greatest new projects in space discovery. There's even a movie on ALMA!
